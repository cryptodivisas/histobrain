## Overview

The Pyramid of the Sun is one of the most impressive monuments in the ancient Americas. Located in the city of Teotihuacan in central Mexico, it dominates the site’s ceremonial core and reflects the extraordinary urban planning of one of Mesoamerica’s greatest civilizations. It remains one of the largest pyramids ever built in the world.

## Historical Context

Teotihuacan flourished long before the Aztec Empire and became a powerful political, religious, and economic center. The Pyramid of the Sun was constructed around the 2nd century AD, during the city’s rise. Although the people who built Teotihuacan left no clearly readable written history, archaeologists know that the city’s influence extended far beyond the Valley of Mexico. The pyramid likely played a major role in public ritual and religious life.

## Structure and Design

Built in a stepped form from earth, stone, and adobe, the Pyramid of the Sun rises dramatically along the Avenue of the Dead, Teotihuacan’s main ceremonial axis. Its location and scale suggest careful planning and symbolic importance. The pyramid is aligned in relation to other major structures in the city, including the Pyramid of the Moon. Excavations have revealed tunnels and offerings associated with sacred use, reinforcing the idea that the monument was tied to cosmology and state power.

## Legacy

The Pyramid of the Sun remains a defining icon of pre-Columbian architecture. It helps scholars understand the sophistication of Teotihuacan and challenges outdated assumptions about the ancient Americas. For visitors, it is a dramatic reminder of the scale and ambition of Mesoamerican civilization. Its massive form continues to inspire interest in archaeology, astronomy, and ancient religion.
